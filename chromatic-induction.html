The Impossible Color Recipe

What makes a color appear to glow beyond its physical saturation limit. Five perceptual mechanisms, the correct hue angle (it's not what you assumed), per-hue optimal lightness from Munsell home values, and spatial parameters from vision science. With CSS implementation for wide-gamut OLED screens.

Five Compounding Mechanisms

The glow effect is not one phenomenon — it's five perceptual mechanisms stacking multiplicatively. Each is independently validated. On a wide-gamut OLED in dark ambient, all five fire simultaneously.

01
Simultaneous Chromatic Contrast
+hue shift
Visual cortex displaces target color away from surround along the opponent-color vector. 180° in OKLCH maximizes chroma boost (radial displacement), not hue shift.
Chevreul 1839 · Hering 1878 · Klauke & Wachtler, JoV 2015
02
Helmholtz–Kohlrausch Effect
2–2.5×
Saturated colors appear brighter than equiluminant white. Strongest for red-magenta (~330° CIELAB) and blue (~270°). Almost no effect for yellow. Amplified on OLED.
Fairchild & Pirrotta, CRA 1991 · Park, JID 2022 · High et al., CRA 2023
03
Hunt Effect
+colorfulness
Perceived colorfulness increases with luminance. CIECAM02 models as M = C × F_L^0.25. Bright key color = more colorful key color. Stack with dark surround for maximum contrast.
Hunt, JOSA 1952 · CIECAM02 standard
04
Gamut Expansion Effect
+chroma
Uniform chromatic surrounds boost perceived target saturation beyond what variegated surrounds achieve. Clean single-color backgrounds work harder than textured ones.
Ekroll, Faul & Wendt, Vision Research 2011
05
Chromatic Adaptation
hyperbolic
Prolonged viewing of a chromatic surround causes the visual system to "discount" it. Target then appears to exceed the physical gamut — a "hyperbolic color." True impossible color territory.
Churchland, Phil. Psychology 2005 · Valberg, Vision Research 1994

Correcting the Hue Angle — 180° Is Right, But Not Why You Think

Key research finding
Klauke & Wachtler (JoV, 2015) and Ratnasingam & Anderson (JoV, 2017) both confirmed: maximum chromatic induction does not occur at 180°. Induction peaks at intermediate hue angles (~60–120° from target) and actually decreases for larger differences, reaching a plateau near full complement. At exactly 180°, the displacement vector is purely radial — pushing the target outward in chroma space rather than rotating its hue. For glow, this is precisely what you want: chroma amplification, not hue shift. 180° in OKLCH is correct — but for radial displacement, not peak induction strength.
If you want to shift the hue
60–120°

Use an intermediate hue angle. Induction peaks here — the target color rotates toward the complement of the surround. Useful for making a neutral appear chromatic, or shifting perceived hue without changing the physical color.

If you want glow — chroma amplification
180° in OKLCH

The full complement maximizes radial displacement in chroma space. The target is pushed outward — it appears more saturated, not hue-shifted. This is the mechanism behind the "impossible color" effect. Use OKLCH, not HSL — HSL's hue circle distorts in the blue-purple region.

Why OKLCH over HSL: HSL is not perceptually uniform. A 180° rotation in HSL from orange lands at blue — but perceptually these are not equally distant complements. In OKLCH, 180° gives the true perceptual complement for each hue. The blue-purple region is most distorted in HSL. All angle specifications in this recipe are OKLCH.

Per-Hue Optimal Lightness — Munsell Home Values

Every hue achieves maximum physical chroma at a different lightness. Munsell called this the "home value" (Book of Color, 1929). Display a color at its home value before applying the surround — this is your highest possible starting chroma before perceptual amplification.

Two Surround Strategies — Choose Your Compound

Gordon & Shapley (2006) demonstrated that luminance contrast inhibits chromatic induction. Pure chromatic contrast (equiluminance) produces the strongest simultaneous contrast effect. But on screens, luminance effects compound powerfully with chromatic effects. Two strategies, each scientifically distinct:

Strategy A
Equiluminant Surround

Match surround L to key L exactly. Push surround C to gamut boundary at complement hue. Gordon & Shapley: equiluminance maximizes pure chromatic induction — the strongest simultaneous contrast mechanism. No luminance competition, pure hue-vs-hue.

Best for: making calm, muted key colors glow on mid-tone backgrounds. The image that started this exploration uses this strategy.

BG: oklch(same L as key · C:0.06–0.12 · H+180°)
Strategy B — recommended for screens
Dark Chromatic Surround

Set surround significantly darker (L 0.15–0.28), with moderate chroma at complement hue. Sacrifices some pure chromatic induction but compounds Hunt effect (colorfulness at high relative luminance) + H-K effect (key appears brighter) + luminance contrast simultaneously.

Best for: maximum perceived glow on OLED displays. The compound effect exceeds what equiluminance achieves perceptually.

BG: oklch(0.18–0.28 · C:0.06–0.14 · H+180°)
The middle path — what worked in practice
The pairs that worked hardest across our tests used a mid-tone surround at L 0.58–0.66 — not matching key lightness, not dark. This is slightly lighter than the equiluminant position but carries enough luminance separation to trigger the Hunt effect while still delivering strong chromatic induction. Surround C at 0.06–0.12 in OKLCH (equivalent to HSL S:28–38%). This is the region that produces "impossible color" percepts from the original image.

Spatial Parameters From Vision Science

Target area
5–10%
Of total composition. Kellner & Wachtler (2016): smaller targets are more susceptible to surround influence. Below 5% loses visual mass; above 15% target begins resisting induction.
Diameter ratio
≥ 3:1
Surround to target. Area ratio ≥ 9:1. Ejima & Takahashi (1983): induction saturates around this ratio. Beyond 5:1 yields diminishing returns but remains effective.
Edge type
Hard
Sharp, clean boundary. At >2–4 cycles/degree (fine stripes), contrast flips to assimilation — target shifts toward surround. For glow: keep edges hard and features large.
Surround texture
None
Uniform solid color only. Shevell & Wei (1998): chromatic contrast in remote regions attenuates induction through contrast gain control. Isolate the pair completely.

The Recipe — Step by Step

  1. Choose key hue. Identify its Munsell home value lightness. Look up the hue in the table above. Set OKLCH L to the home value range — this is your maximum physical chroma starting point. Never deviate downward from home value to soften a color. The hue angle determines character. The saturation is non-negotiable. Red-orange (~8°) is the canonical example. Cyan (~188°) is the strongest cool-hue equivalent.
  2. Push key chroma to gamut boundary. In OKLCH, increase C until you hit the sRGB gamut edge (colors become invalid/clamp). Pull back 0.01–0.02 for safety. For Display P3, the boundary extends roughly 25–40% further — most significant for greens, reds, and magentas. Check H-K column: red-magenta and blue hues compound glow through perceived brightness beyond their measured luminance.
  3. Compute surround hue: key H + 180° in OKLCH. This is the true perceptual complement. The 180° displacement maximizes radial chroma amplification — pushing the key color outward in color space. Not the angle of maximum induction strength, but the angle of maximum chroma boost for an already-chromatic key.
  4. Set surround lightness. Choose your strategy. Strategy A (equiluminant): match surround L to key L exactly — maximum pure chromatic induction. Strategy B (dark surround): L 0.18–0.28 — compounds Hunt and H-K effects on screen. The pragmatic middle: L 0.58–0.66 — mid-tone, lighter than the key, delivers strong induction plus partial Hunt effect. This is the "impossible color" region from the original image.
  5. Set surround chroma: C 0.06–0.12 in OKLCH. Below 0.06 (≈ HSL S:18%) you lose chromatic charge — simultaneous contrast weakens. Above 0.14 (≈ HSL S:42%) the surround begins competing for attention. The window is narrow. This is equivalent to HSL S:26–38% in the mid-tone range. The surround must feel flat — the key color must feel impossible.
  6. Apply spatial constraints. Target: 5–10% of total area. Surround diameter ≥ 3× target. Edges: hard, no gradient, no anti-aliasing at the boundary. Surround: uniform solid, no texture, no competing chromatic elements anywhere in the frame. Isolation is everything.
  7. Apply Bezold-Brücke compensation for warm hues at high brightness. Reds and oranges displayed at peak brightness shift toward yellow perceptually. If displaying a red key at maximum luminance, start the hue 5–8° more toward red-magenta than the target percept. Blues shift toward blue — typically beneficial for glow. Four invariant hues need no compensation: 478nm (blue), 503nm (green), 572nm (yellow), extraspectral purple.
  8. Implement with P3 media query. Use OKLCH throughout. Add a P3 override that pushes key C further into the wide gamut. Target OLED displays and dark ambient environments for maximum compound effect. HDR peak luminance (1000+ cd/m²) on target further amplifies Hunt and H-K mechanisms.

CSS Implementation

/* BASE — sRGB fallback */ .surround { background: oklch(0.62 0.09 205); /* mid-tone teal: L=.62, C=.09, H=205° */ } .key { background: oklch(0.55 0.22 25); /* red at home lightness, max sRGB chroma */ } /* P3 OVERRIDE — wider gamut, stronger glow */ @media (color-gamut: p3) { .surround { background: oklch(0.60 0.12 205); /* more chroma in surround, P3 can hold it */ } .key { background: oklch(0.55 0.28 25); /* push chroma beyond sRGB boundary */ } } /* SPATIAL — enforce isolation */ .composition { position: relative; isolation: isolate; /* no bleed from outside elements */ } .key { width: 50%; /* target ~5-10% of total area, adjust per layout */ height: 60%; image-rendering: pixelated; /* hard edges — no subpixel softening */ }

What Destroys the Effect

  • HSL for computing complements. HSL is not perceptually uniform — 180° in HSL is not the true perceptual complement. The blue-purple region is most distorted. Use OKLCH.
  • Dark surrounds without H-K hues. Strategy B (dark surround) works best with H-K-strong key colors: red-magenta, blue, purple. For orange or yellow keys, H-K is minimal — dark surround loses more chromatic induction than it gains from the compound effect. Use equiluminant or mid-tone surround.
  • Pulling key saturation down for "mood." Mood comes from hue angle. Saturation is the explosion. Rose, lavender, mint, denim can all feel calm at full OKLCH chroma because the hue character is calm — not because the chroma is reduced.
  • Surround chroma above C:0.14 in OKLCH. The surround starts competing. The key's monopoly on saturation breaks down. Induction weakens as surround and target become perceptually similar.
  • Soft edges or gradients at the boundary. Above 2–4 spatial cycles per degree, contrast flips to assimilation — the target shifts toward the surround instead of away. Keep the hard edge. No blurs, no feathering.
  • Textured or variegated surrounds. Remote chromatic contrast (outside the immediate surround) attenuates induction through contrast gain control (Shevell & Wei, 1998). Isolate the pair completely from all other chromatic elements.
  • Multiple saturated elements. Each one dilutes the key's optical monopoly. One key color per composition. One explosion.
  • Flat L:0.52 for all hues. Greens peak at L:0.55–0.62. Blues at L:0.38–0.50. Purples at L:0.52–0.62. Yellows at L:0.82–0.90. A universal lightness actively kills the chroma of half your palette.
  • Bright ambient light. Reduces effective display contrast ratio, weakens H-K effect, weakens Hunt effect. The compound mechanisms are environment-dependent. Dark room amplifies everything.
